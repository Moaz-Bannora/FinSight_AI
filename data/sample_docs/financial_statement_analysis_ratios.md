# Financial Statement Analysis and Ratio Interpretation Notes

## Purpose

Financial statement analysis studies a company's income statement, balance sheet, and cash-flow statement to understand profitability, liquidity, leverage, efficiency, and risk.

## Income Statement

The income statement explains performance over a period. It shows revenue, expenses, operating profit, interest, taxes, and net income.

Important questions:

- Is revenue growing?
- Are gross margins stable or improving?
- Are operating expenses controlled?
- Is net income positive and sustainable?
- Are one-time gains or losses affecting profit?

## Balance Sheet

The balance sheet shows financial position at a point in time. It reports assets, liabilities, and equity.

Important questions:

- Does the company have enough current assets to meet short-term obligations?
- Is debt increasing faster than equity?
- Are inventories or receivables growing too quickly?
- Is the company dependent on short-term borrowing?

## Cash-Flow Statement

The cash-flow statement shows how cash moves through operating, investing, and financing activities.

Operating cash flow is especially important because a company can report accounting profit while still facing cash pressure.

## Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios measure the ability to meet short-term obligations.

Current ratio:

$$Current\ Ratio=\frac{Current\ Assets}{Current\ Liabilities}$$

Quick ratio:

$$Quick\ Ratio=\frac{Cash+Marketable\ Securities+Receivables}{Current\ Liabilities}$$

A current ratio above 1.0 usually means current assets exceed current liabilities. However, very high current ratios may also indicate inefficient use of assets.

## Leverage Ratios

Leverage ratios measure dependence on debt financing.

Debt-to-equity:

$$Debt\text{-}to\text{-}Equity=\frac{Total\ Debt}{Total\ Equity}$$

Interest coverage:

$$Interest\ Coverage=\frac{EBIT}{Interest\ Expense}$$

High leverage can increase returns when business conditions are good, but it also increases financial risk during downturns.

## Profitability Ratios

Profitability ratios measure how efficiently a company turns sales, assets, or equity into profit.

Gross margin:

$$Gross\ Margin=\frac{Revenue-COGS}{Revenue}\times100$$

Net profit margin:

$$Net\ Margin=\frac{Net\ Income}{Revenue}\times100$$

Return on equity:

$$ROE=\frac{Net\ Income}{Average\ Equity}\times100$$

Profitability should be compared across time and against industry peers.

## Efficiency Ratios

Efficiency ratios measure how well a company uses assets and working capital.

Inventory turnover:

$$Inventory\ Turnover=\frac{COGS}{Average\ Inventory}$$

Receivables turnover:

$$Receivables\ Turnover=\frac{Revenue}{Average\ Accounts\ Receivable}$$

Slow inventory turnover or slow receivables collection can create cash pressure.

## Common Interpretation Mistakes

- Looking at one ratio alone.
- Comparing companies from different industries without adjustment.
- Ignoring seasonality.
- Ignoring accounting policy differences.
- Treating positive profit as proof of strong liquidity.
- Ignoring risk disclosures and management commentary.

## Strong Analysis Structure

A good financial statement answer should include:

- What the ratio or trend shows.
- Why it matters.
- Whether it is improving or worsening.
- How it compares with peers or benchmarks.
- What limitation affects the interpretation.
