# Cash Flow and Working Capital Management Notes

## Purpose

Cash-flow analysis studies whether a company can generate enough cash to operate, invest, repay debt, and return capital to owners. Working capital management focuses on short-term assets and liabilities such as inventory, receivables, payables, and cash.

## Profit Versus Cash Flow

A company can be profitable but still face liquidity pressure. Profit is based on accounting rules, while cash flow tracks actual cash inflows and outflows.

Example:

- A company sells goods on credit and records revenue immediately.
- Cash may not arrive until customers pay.
- If inventory and receivables grow quickly, operating cash flow can become weak even when profit is positive.

## Operating Cash Flow

Operating cash flow measures cash generated by core business operations. Strong operating cash flow supports debt repayment, reinvestment, and resilience.

Key warning signs:

- Net income is positive but operating cash flow is negative.
- Receivables grow faster than revenue.
- Inventory grows faster than sales.
- Payables are stretched unusually far.

## Cash Conversion Cycle

The cash conversion cycle estimates how long cash is tied up in operations:

$$Cash\ Conversion\ Cycle=Days\ Inventory+Days\ Receivables-Days\ Payables$$

A shorter cash conversion cycle usually means cash returns faster. A longer cycle may indicate working capital pressure.

## Inventory Days

Inventory days measures how long inventory is held before being sold:

$$Inventory\ Days=\frac{Average\ Inventory}{COGS}\times365$$

Rising inventory days may suggest slowing demand, overstocking, supply chain problems, or obsolete inventory risk.

## Receivables Days

Receivables days measures how long customers take to pay:

$$Receivables\ Days=\frac{Average\ Accounts\ Receivable}{Revenue}\times365$$

Rising receivables days may indicate collection problems or looser credit terms.

## Payables Days

Payables days measures how long the company takes to pay suppliers:

$$Payables\ Days=\frac{Average\ Accounts\ Payable}{COGS}\times365$$

Higher payables days can preserve cash temporarily, but very high values may signal supplier pressure.

## Free Cash Flow

Free cash flow measures cash remaining after capital expenditures:

$$Free\ Cash\ Flow=Operating\ Cash\ Flow-Capital\ Expenditure$$

Positive free cash flow gives a company more flexibility. Negative free cash flow may be acceptable for growth investments, but it must be financed.

## Working Capital Improvement Ideas

- Improve customer collection processes.
- Review credit terms.
- Reduce obsolete or slow-moving inventory.
- Improve demand forecasting.
- Negotiate supplier terms carefully.
- Avoid cutting inventory so much that operations suffer.

## Interpretation Guide

Working capital analysis should connect ratios with business reality. A high cash conversion cycle is not automatically bad, but it deserves explanation. Industry norms, seasonality, growth stage, and supply chain conditions all matter.
