# Valuation and Cost of Capital Notes

## Purpose

Valuation estimates what an asset, project, or company is worth. The value depends on expected cash flows, growth, risk, and the discount rate.

## Discounted Cash Flow Valuation

Discounted Cash Flow, or DCF, values an asset by discounting expected future free cash flows:

$$Value=\sum_{t=1}^{n}\frac{FCF_t}{(1+r)^t}+\frac{Terminal\ Value}{(1+r)^n}$$

Where:

- $FCF_t$ is free cash flow in year $t$.
- $r$ is the discount rate.
- Terminal value estimates cash flows beyond the explicit forecast period.

## Free Cash Flow

Free cash flow is cash available after operating costs, taxes, reinvestment, and working capital needs.

One common simplified formula is:

$$FCF=EBIT(1-Tax\ Rate)+Depreciation-Capital\ Expenditure-\Delta Working\ Capital$$

Free cash flow is different from net income because accounting profit includes non-cash items and accruals.

## Cost of Equity

Cost of equity is the required return expected by shareholders. A common model is CAPM:

$$Cost\ of\ Equity=Risk\text{-}free\ Rate+\beta(Market\ Return-Risk\text{-}free\ Rate)$$

Higher beta usually means higher systematic risk and a higher required return.

## Cost of Debt

Cost of debt is the effective borrowing cost. Because interest is often tax-deductible, analysts often use after-tax cost of debt:

$$After\text{-}tax\ Cost\ of\ Debt=Pre\text{-}tax\ Cost\ of\ Debt\times(1-Tax\ Rate)$$

## Weighted Average Cost of Capital

Weighted Average Cost of Capital, or WACC, combines the cost of equity and after-tax cost of debt:

$$WACC=\frac{E}{D+E}R_e+\frac{D}{D+E}R_d(1-T)$$

Where:

- $E$ is market value of equity.
- $D$ is market value of debt.
- $R_e$ is cost of equity.
- $R_d$ is cost of debt.
- $T$ is tax rate.

WACC is commonly used as a discount rate for projects with risk similar to the overall firm.

## Terminal Value

Terminal value estimates value after the explicit forecast period.

Gordon growth terminal value:

$$Terminal\ Value=\frac{FCF_{n+1}}{WACC-g}$$

The growth rate $g$ should usually be conservative and not exceed the long-term growth rate of the economy.

## Relative Valuation

Relative valuation compares companies using multiples.

Common multiples:

- Price-to-earnings ratio.
- Enterprise value to EBITDA.
- Price-to-sales ratio.
- Price-to-book ratio.

Multiples are quick and market-based, but they depend heavily on peer selection and accounting comparability.

## Valuation Risks

Valuation is sensitive to assumptions. Small changes in discount rate, growth rate, margins, reinvestment, or terminal value can materially change the final estimate.

## Interpretation Guide

A good valuation answer should explain the valuation method, key assumptions, sensitivity to assumptions, and why the result should be treated as an estimate rather than a precise fact.
